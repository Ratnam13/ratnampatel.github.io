Finite Element Analysis of Welded Pressure-Vessel Nozzle

Conducted finite-element stress analysis of a welded nozzle–flange joint on a pressure vessel subjected to agitator-induced loading and internal pressure, under ASME Section VIII, Division 1 compliance. This work was completed during my internship at Viram Technologies Enterprises in Pune.

Project Summary

As part of a six-week internship at Viram Technologies Enterprises, I assisted in the simulation-driven validation of pressure-vessel components. The project aimed to ensure weld and flange integrity under combined mechanical and pressure loading, meeting ASME BPVC requirements through detailed finite-element analysis and CAD-validated geometry.

Engineering Objective & Methodology

The goal was to assess the structural safety of a stainless-steel nozzle welded to a vessel dish end, subjected to a rotating agitator’s bending moment, torque, and internal pressure. The workflow involved:

  1. Reconstruction of the nozzle–flange–dish geometry in CAD from fabrication drawings.
  2. Meshing and refinement in ANSYS Workbench with tetrahedral solid elements.
  3. Application of combined loading conditions and constraint setup to replicate real supports.
  4. Post-processing of stress, deformation, and reaction forces to ensure equilibrium and compliance.

Simulation Setup & Boundary Conditions

  • Material: Stainless Steel 316L (SA-240 / SA-312 / SA-182)
  • Elastic Modulus: 181 GPa
  • Poisson’s Ratio: 0.31
  • Density: 8027 kg/m³

Applied loads included:

  • Agitator force: 4009 kgf
  • Torsional moment: 1043 kg·m
  • Internal pressure: 3.2 bar

Boundary conditions constrained the dish end tangentially and axially to emulate the vessel wall’s stiffness. A refined mesh was generated near the weld region to capture stress gradients, while the remaining geometry used a coarser grid for efficiency.

Results & Validation

  • Maximum deformation: 0.452 mm
  • Maximum Von Mises stress: 36.1 MPa
  • Allowable stress (ASME): 70 MPa
  • Mesh refinement: Stress variation < 1.5% upon 35% element increase
  • Global equilibrium: Reaction forces = 39.3 kN (≈ applied load)

The results confirmed that all failure-prevention conditions were satisfied with a minimum safety factor above 2.9. Both deformation and stress levels were significantly below ASME allowable limits. Verification through inspection data of fabricated components showed strong correlation with simulation predictions.

Key Findings

  • The welded nozzle design is structurally safe under operational loads and internal pressure.
  • All stress limits comply with ASME Section VIII criteria.
  • Mesh independence and global equilibrium confirm numerical accuracy.
  • Simulation-based validation matched physical inspection trends, reinforcing model fidelity.
