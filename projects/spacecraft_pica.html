CFD Analysis of Re-Entry Spacecraft with PICA TPS

Density-based transient CFD of hypersonic external flow over a blunt-body capsule outfitted with Phenolic Impregnated Carbon Ablator (PICA) tiles. I modeled the coupled fluid–solid domains, generated quality meshes per region, and analyzed velocity streamlines and temperature fields under re-entry conditions. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Project Summary

The analysis evaluates PICA-tiled thermal protection on a simplified re-entry geometry under high-speed air flow. A multi-domain model (outer fluid, inner tile layer, and internal solid) was meshed with region-specific resolutions and solved using a density-based transient scheme with Spalart–Allmaras turbulence to capture hypersonic features. Post-processing focused on velocity streamlines over the tiles and temperature distributions on the forebody. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

  • TPS: PICA tiles (ρ=0.32 g/cc, k≈0.29 W·m⁻¹·K⁻¹, cp≈2.1 kJ·kg⁻¹·K⁻¹)
  • Flow: hypersonic air (ideal gas, Sutherland viscosity)
  • Solver: density-based, transient; turbulence: Spalart–Allmaras

Background & Objectives

Re-entry vehicles experience extreme aerodynamic heating; Thermal Protection Systems (TPS) such as PICA must reject or absorb heat to maintain structural integrity. The objective was to simulate external hypersonic flow over a simplified capsule and characterize velocity and temperature fields with PICA tiles applied to the forebody. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Geometry & Materials

Three domains were constructed in ANSYS DesignModeler using boolean operations: an outer fluid domain (2 m × 1 m × 1 m), an inner “tile” domain (0.3 m cube) representing the PICA layer, and an interior solid cube (0.18 m) representing the payload enclosure. Air was assigned to the fluid; the tile layer used PICA properties; the interior solid used aluminum. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

Meshing Strategy

Structured/quad-dominant meshing with progressively finer element sizing near the capsule was used to resolve shock and boundary-layer gradients. Representative settings: outer domain Δ≈1 cm, tile layer Δ≈5 mm, inner solid Δ≈1 mm. Mesh metrics: orthogonal quality > 0.8 on the outer domain; low skewness overall. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Hypersonic CFD Setup

  • Flow conditions: Inlet air velocity ≈ 12,500 m·s⁻¹; density-based transient solver for hypersonic compressibility. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}
  • Thermal boundary: Front face of the tile domain at 2800 °C; initial inner face ≈ 50 °C. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
  • Turbulence: Spalart–Allmaras for high-speed external aerodynamics. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • Models: Ideal-gas air with Sutherland viscosity; coupled fluid–solid energy transfer across interfaces. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

Results

  • Velocity streamlines: Blunt-body flowfield with bow-shock-like behavior and expected deflection over the tiles; realistic external pattern achieved once the inner domain was treated as solid. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
  • Temperature contours: Forebody temperatures reported over the 50–70 °C range for the displayed case, noting deviations from earlier work due to changed inner-domain and boundary assumptions. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Key Findings

  • PICA offers effective thermal protection under the modeled hypersonic condition; ablation physics were not included and would further increase realism. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}
  • Treating the inner domain as solid (tiles) produces a physically consistent external flow pattern over the forebody. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
  • Geometric fidelity (e.g., domed forebody) is expected to improve quantitative agreement with canonical re-entry data in future runs. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

My Contributions
