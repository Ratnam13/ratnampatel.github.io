CFD Simulation for Microdroplet Generator

Project Summary

The study explores controlled droplet formation in microchannels using immiscible fluids (water continuous phase, castor-oil dispersed). By sweeping inlet velocities and geometry refinements, I established a stable dripping regime and demonstrated tunable droplet size and coalescence behavior consistent with literature-reported Capillary-number scaling.

  • Domain: Microfluidics (lab-on-chip)
  • Physics: Multiphase laminar flow; interfacial tension
  • Goal: Stable generation + predictable droplet size

Background & Governing Physics

Droplet breakup arises from the competition between viscous shear and surface tension. The non-dimensional Capillary number, \( Ca = \mu U / \sigma \), captures this balance for the continuous phase. As \( Ca \) increases (or as the water-to-oil velocity ratio rises), droplet diameter decreases and the regime shifts from squeezing to dripping and eventually jetting.

  • Capillary number
  • Interfacial tension
  • Flow-focusing junctions
  • Regime transitions

Method & Numerical Setup

  1. Geometry: Flow-focusing junction; 2D and 3D variants tested to evaluate stability and cost.
  2. Fluids: Water (continuous), castor oil (dispersed); material properties set to representative values for microflows.
  3. Inlets: Velocity-specified; swept water:oil ratios to modulate \( Ca \) and shear.
  4. Solver: Laminar, multiphase CFD with interface-capturing; mesh refined at the junction and outlet throat.
  5. Outputs: Droplet diameter, spacing, regime identification; coalescence control via flow-rate timing.

Simulation Observations

  • 3D trials: Early configurations showed backflow/instability at the outlet; corrected via outlet resizing and refined meshing.
  • 2D stabilization: Stable co-flow achieved; droplet formation required lowering the dispersed-phase velocity.
